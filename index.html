Chapter 1: The Beginning of Change

The digital revolution began in earnest during the latter half of the 20th century, marking a profound shift from mechanical and analog electronic technology to digital electronics. This transition fundamentally altered how humans interact with information and with each other.

In the early days of computing, machines were enormous, expensive, and accessible only to governments, large corporations, and academic institutions. The ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer), completed in 1945, weighed more than 30 tons and occupied a room larger than most modern apartments.

But the trajectory of computing would change dramatically with the invention of the transistor in 1947 by Bell Laboratories scientists John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley. This tiny component would eventually replace the bulky vacuum tubes that powered early computers, setting the stage for miniaturization.
